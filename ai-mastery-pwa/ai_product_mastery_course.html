Session 01 · Week 1

What AI Actually Is (and Isn't)

Duration: 30-40 minutes · Day 1

1.1 The Big Picture

Artificial Intelligence is not one thing — it's an umbrella term for machines performing tasks that typically require human intelligence. The confusion in the market comes from people using "AI" to mean very different things. As a product person, your job is to cut through the noise.

The AI hierarchy — think of it as nesting dolls:

Artificial Intelligence (broadest — any smart-seeming system)
  └── Machine Learning (systems that learn from data)
        └── Deep Learning (ML using neural networks with many layers)
              └── Generative AI (deep learning that creates new content)
                    └── Large Language Models (GenAI specialized in text)

1.2 Types of AI That Matter for Products

Predictive AI
The "Old" AI — Still Massively Valuable
Takes input data → predicts an outcome. Used in Netflix recommendations, fraud detection, demand forecasting.
Corporate Example

Amazon's supply chain uses predictive AI to pre-position inventory in warehouses before you even order. This saves billions in logistics costs.

"Given X, what's likely Y?"
Generative AI
The "New" AI — What Everyone's Excited About
Takes a prompt/input → creates new content (text, image, code, audio). Examples: ChatGPT, Claude, Midjourney, GitHub Copilot.
Corporate Example

Klarna replaced 700 customer service agents with an AI assistant handling 2.3 million conversations in its first month (2024). It does the same work in 2 minutes that used to take 11 minutes.

"Given this instruction, create something new."
Analytical AI
The Workhorse Nobody Talks About
Processes and extracts insights from large datasets. Used in business intelligence, anomaly detection, document parsing.
Corporate Example

JPMorgan's COiN platform analyzes commercial loan agreements in seconds — work that used to take lawyers 360,000 hours annually.

"Given this pile of data, what's important?"

1.3 What AI Is NOT

This is critical for your credibility as a product person. You must be the one in the room who doesn't overpromise.

✕
AI is not "thinking."
It's pattern matching at massive scale. LLMs don't understand meaning — they predict the most statistically likely next token.
✕
AI is not reliable by default.
Models hallucinate (confidently produce wrong information). This is not a bug being fixed — it's inherent to how probabilistic models work.
✕
AI is not a replacement for product strategy.
"Add AI" is not a strategy. The question is always: what specific user problem does this solve better than the alternative?
✕
AI is not magic.
Behind every impressive demo is careful data curation, prompt engineering, guardrails, fallback systems, and human oversight.

1.4 The Key Terms You Must Know

Term Plain English Why It Matters
Model A trained AI system (like GPT-4, Claude, Gemini) You choose which model fits your product's needs
Training The process of teaching a model using data Determines what the model knows and its biases
Inference When a trained model processes new input and gives output This is what your users experience — and what you pay for
Parameters Internal "knobs" a model adjusts during training (billions of them) More parameters ≠ always better. Impacts cost and speed
Hallucination AI generates confident but factually wrong output Your #1 product risk with GenAI
Fine-tuning Additional training on specific data to specialize a model How you make a general model work for your domain
Prompt The input/instruction you give to a model The interface between user intent and AI output
Token A chunk of text (~4 characters or ~¾ of a word) How AI companies charge you and how context limits work
Latency Time between sending input and receiving output Directly impacts user experience
Guardrails Rules/filters that prevent unwanted AI outputs Essential for production AI products

1.5 A Framework for Thinking About AI in Products

Use this whenever someone pitches you an "AI feature":

⚡ The 3-Question Filter
Is this a prediction problem, a generation problem, or an analysis problem?
This tells you what type of AI you need.
What happens when the AI is wrong?
This tells you the risk level and how much human oversight you need.
What's the non-AI alternative, and is AI meaningfully better?
This prevents you from building AI for AI's sake.
Real-World Application
"Let's add AI to auto-respond to customer support tickets."
1
This is a generation problem (creating response text) combined with prediction (classifying ticket intent).
2
If the AI is wrong, you send a wrong answer to a paying customer — high stakes. You need a human-in-the-loop or high-confidence thresholds.
3
The non-AI alternative is templates + human agents. AI could be meaningfully better at handling repetitive L1 tickets (password resets, order status) but risky for complex complaints.
→ Product decision: Deploy AI for L1 tickets with high-confidence routing, keep humans for everything else. This is roughly what Klarna did.

1.6 The Current State of AI (2024-2025)

📉
Foundation models are commoditizing
GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, Llama — performance gaps are narrowing. The moat is not the model.
🏗️
Value is moving to the application layer
Whoever builds the best product experience on top of these models wins. This is where product people matter.
🚀
Enterprise adoption is early but accelerating
Most companies are in "pilot" or "experiment" phase. Very few have AI in production at scale. This means massive opportunity.
🤖
Agents are the next frontier
AI that doesn't just answer questions but takes actions — books meetings, writes and sends emails, executes multi-step workflows.
💰
Cost of intelligence is dropping fast
What cost $100 in API calls in early 2023 costs ~$1 now for equivalent quality. This unlocks previously unviable use cases.

Session 02 · Week 1

The AI Product Landscape

Duration: 30-40 minutes · Day 2

2.1 Mapping the AI Product Universe

Before you can build or market AI products, you need a mental map of what exists. The AI product landscape is massive, but it follows a clear structure. Understanding this map tells you where the money is, where the competition is, and where the opportunities are.

There are essentially four categories of AI products in the market right now. Every AI company you encounter fits into one of these — and knowing which category you're in shapes everything from your GTM strategy to your pricing model.

2.2 The Four Categories of AI Products

Category 1
AI-Native Products
Products that couldn't exist without AI. The AI is the product. Remove the AI, and there's nothing left.
Examples

ChatGPT / Claude — the entire product is an AI interface. Midjourney — AI image generation. GitHub Copilot — AI code completion. Perplexity — AI-powered search.

Moat: Data flywheels, user habits, brand trust
Category 2
AI-Enhanced Products
Existing products that have added AI features to become better. The product existed before AI — now AI makes it more powerful.
Examples

Notion AI — writing assistance inside an existing note-taking tool. Canva Magic — AI design features in an existing design tool. Salesforce Einstein — AI predictions inside an existing CRM. Gmail Smart Compose — generative text in existing email.

Moat: Existing user base, distribution, workflow lock-in
Category 3
AI Infrastructure & Tooling
Products that help other companies build, deploy, and manage AI. They sell picks and shovels in the gold rush.
Examples

Hugging Face — model hosting and community. LangChain — framework for building LLM applications. Pinecone — vector database for AI search. Scale AI — data labeling and evaluation. AWS Bedrock / Azure AI — cloud AI services.

Moat: Developer ecosystem, switching costs, integrations
Category 4
Vertical AI Solutions
AI products built for a specific industry or use case. They combine domain expertise with AI to solve problems that general-purpose tools can't.
Examples

Harvey — AI for law firms. Abridge — AI for medical documentation. AlphaFold — AI for protein structure prediction. Jasper — AI for marketing content. Moveworks — AI for IT support.

Moat: Domain data, regulatory knowledge, industry trust

Why This Matters for You

When you're job hunting or building products, the category you're in determines your entire strategy. An AI-native product competes on model quality and UX. An AI-enhanced product competes on integration and distribution. AI infrastructure competes on developer experience. Vertical AI competes on domain expertise. Know which game you're playing.

2.3 Who's Building What — The Key Players

The AI landscape has distinct layers, and different types of companies dominate each layer. Here's how to think about who's who:

Layer 1 — Foundation Model Providers (The "Model Makers")

Who: OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, Meta AI, Mistral, Cohere

These companies train the massive base models that everyone else builds on. This is the most capital-intensive layer — training a frontier model can cost $100M+. The competition here is fierce and consolidating. As a product person, you're unlikely to work at this layer unless you join one of these companies directly.

Layer 2 — Cloud & Compute (The "Landlords")

Who: AWS, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud, NVIDIA, CoreWeave

They provide the computing power (GPUs) and cloud infrastructure to train and run AI models. NVIDIA is the undisputed king here — their GPUs power ~90% of AI training. Cloud providers make it easy to deploy models without managing hardware. This is a massive business but not where most product people operate.

Layer 3 — Application Layer (The "Builders" — Where You Want to Be)

Who: Thousands of startups + every SaaS company adding AI features

This is the layer where AI meets users. It's the fastest-growing layer, the most accessible for product people, and where the majority of AI product jobs exist. You take models from Layer 1, run them on Layer 2 infrastructure, and build products that solve real problems. This is your target zone.

2.4 Where the Money Flows

Understanding funding and revenue patterns tells you where the market believes value is being created. Here's the 2024-2025 picture:

💰
Foundation models attracted the biggest checks
OpenAI raised $6.6B at a $157B valuation (2024). Anthropic raised $7.3B+ across multiple rounds. xAI raised $6B. But this is a winner-takes-most game — there's room for maybe 5-7 major model providers globally.
📈
Application-layer startups are where the volume is
Hundreds of well-funded startups building AI products for specific use cases. This is where most new AI product roles are being created. Examples: Glean ($4.6B valuation for enterprise AI search), Cursor (AI code editor), Writer (enterprise AI writing).
🏢
Enterprise spend on AI is the real story
Every Fortune 500 company has an AI budget now. McKinsey estimates AI could add $4.4 trillion annually to the global economy. The biggest spend is on integrating AI into existing workflows — not on flashy chatbots.
⚠️
Revenue vs. hype gap exists
Many AI startups have high valuations but modest revenue. The companies that are actually making money are those with clear enterprise use cases and measurable ROI — not those with the most impressive demos.

2.5 The Build vs. Buy vs. Integrate Decision

Every company adopting AI faces this decision. As a product person, you'll be asked to make this call — or at least influence it. Here's the framework:

🔧 Build vs. Buy vs. Integrate
BUILD your own AI — Train or fine-tune models in-house
When: You have proprietary data that's your competitive advantage, you need full control, and you have the engineering talent. Example: Netflix building its own recommendation engine — their data IS their moat.
BUY an AI product — Purchase a vertical AI solution
When: A specialized vendor solves your exact problem better than you could. You want fast deployment and don't need deep customization. Example: A law firm buying Harvey instead of building their own legal AI.
INTEGRATE AI APIs — Plug foundation models into your product
When: You want AI capabilities without building models. You need flexibility and speed to market. Most common approach for startups. Example: Notion integrating Claude/GPT APIs for writing assistance.
Real-World Decision
"We're a mid-size e-commerce company. Should we build our own AI chatbot for customer support?"
1
BUILD? Probably not. You'd need an ML team of 5-10 people ($2M+/year). Unless customer support is your core differentiator, this is overkill.
2
BUY? Strong option. Products like Intercom Fin, Zendesk AI, or Ada already have pre-built customer support AI. Deployment in weeks, not months.
3
INTEGRATE? Good middle ground. Use Claude or GPT APIs, feed in your product catalog and FAQ data via RAG, build a custom interface. More control than BUY, cheaper than BUILD.
→ Product decision: Most mid-size companies should start with BUY or INTEGRATE. Only BUILD when AI is your core product or when you have unique data that gives you a genuine advantage.

2.6 Market Trends You Must Track

These are the five shifts shaping the AI product landscape right now. Each one creates new product opportunities:

1️⃣
From chatbots to agents
The market is moving beyond "ask AI a question" to "AI does the work for you." Products like Devin (AI software engineer), Operator (OpenAI's computer-using agent), and enterprise workflow agents are the frontier. This changes how you design product UX — from chat interfaces to task-completion interfaces.
2️⃣
From general to specialized
General-purpose AI (ChatGPT, Claude) is powerful but vertical-specific AI products are capturing enterprise budgets. A legal AI that understands case law beats a general chatbot every time for a law firm. Specialization = higher willingness to pay.
3️⃣
From single-model to multi-model
Smart products are routing different tasks to different models. Use a fast, cheap model for simple tasks and a powerful, expensive model for complex ones. This is called "model routing" and it dramatically reduces costs while maintaining quality.
4️⃣
From cloud to edge
Smaller AI models are running directly on devices (phones, laptops). Apple Intelligence, on-device Gemini Nano, and local LLMs mean some AI features work without internet. This opens new product categories around privacy and speed.
5️⃣
From "AI feature" to "AI-first redesign"
The first wave was adding AI buttons to existing products. The next wave is rethinking entire products around AI capabilities. Example: Cursor didn't add AI to VS Code — they redesigned the entire code editor experience around AI being the primary collaborator.

2.7 A Framework for Evaluating Any AI Product

When you encounter a new AI product — as a user, a competitor, or a potential employer — use this evaluation lens:

🔍 The AI Product Evaluation Grid
Which of the 4 categories is this? (Native, Enhanced, Infrastructure, Vertical)
This tells you their competitive dynamics and go-to-market strategy.
What's their moat? (Data, distribution, domain expertise, user habit, or nothing?)
If the answer is "we use GPT-4" — they have no moat. Anyone can do that.
Who pays, and why can't they just use ChatGPT directly?
The "ChatGPT wrapper" problem. If your product is just a prettier interface on top of a foundation model, you're vulnerable.
What happens when models get better and cheaper?
Some AI products become more valuable as models improve (platforms). Others become obsolete (thin wrappers). Which one is this?
Apply the Grid — Grammarly
"Evaluate Grammarly as an AI product using the grid."
1
Category: AI-Enhanced product (it started as rule-based grammar checking, now heavily AI-powered with generative rewriting features).
2
Moat: Distribution (30M daily users, browser extension installed everywhere, deep integrations into workflows). Plus years of writing data to fine-tune on.
3
Why not just use ChatGPT? Grammarly works inside your existing tools — Gmail, Docs, Slack. You don't have to copy-paste into a separate window. That seamless integration is the product value.
4
When models improve? Threat is real. As native AI gets better in Gmail and Google Docs, Grammarly's value proposition erodes. They're counter-moving by becoming an "AI communication assistant" — more than just grammar.
→ Assessment: Strong product with real moat (distribution), but under platform risk as OS-level AI improves. A good case study of how AI-enhanced products must keep evolving.
