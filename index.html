Session 01 · Week 1

What AI Actually Is (and Isn't)

30-40 minutes · Day 1

1.1 The Big Picture

Artificial Intelligence is not one thing — it's an umbrella term for machines performing tasks that typically require human intelligence. The confusion in the market comes from people using "AI" to mean very different things. As a product person, your job is to cut through the noise.

The AI hierarchy — think of it as nesting dolls:

Artificial Intelligence (broadest — any smart-seeming system)
  └── Machine Learning (systems that learn from data)
      └── Deep Learning (ML using neural networks with many layers)
          └── Generative AI (deep learning that creates new content)
              └── Large Language Models (GenAI specialized in text)

1.2 Types of AI That Matter for Products

Predictive AI
The "Old" AI — Still Massively Valuable
Takes input data → predicts an outcome. Used in Netflix recommendations, fraud detection, demand forecasting.
Corporate Example

Amazon's supply chain uses predictive AI to pre-position inventory in warehouses before you even order. Saves billions in logistics.

"Given X, what's likely Y?"
Generative AI
The "New" AI — What Everyone's Excited About
Takes a prompt → creates new content (text, image, code, audio). Examples: ChatGPT, Claude, Midjourney, GitHub Copilot.
Corporate Example

Klarna replaced 700 CS agents with AI handling 2.3M conversations in month one. 2 min vs 11 min per conversation.

"Given this instruction, create something new."
Analytical AI
The Workhorse Nobody Talks About
Processes and extracts insights from large datasets. Used in BI dashboards, anomaly detection, document parsing.
Corporate Example

JPMorgan's COiN analyzes commercial loan agreements in seconds — work that took lawyers 360,000 hours annually.

"Given this pile of data, what's important?"

1.3 What AI Is NOT

Critical for your credibility. Be the person in the room who doesn't overpromise.

✕
AI is not "thinking."
It's pattern matching at scale. LLMs predict the next token, they don't understand meaning.
✕
AI is not reliable by default.
Models hallucinate. This is inherent to probabilistic systems, not a bug being fixed.
✕
AI is not a replacement for product strategy.
"Add AI" is not a strategy. What specific problem does it solve better than the alternative?
✕
AI is not magic.
Behind every demo: data curation, prompt engineering, guardrails, fallbacks, and human oversight.

1.5 The 3-Question Filter

Use this whenever someone pitches an "AI feature":

⚡The 3-Question Filter
Is this a prediction, generation, or analysis problem?
Tells you what type of AI you need.
What happens when the AI is wrong?
Tells you risk level and oversight needed.
What's the non-AI alternative, and is AI meaningfully better?
Prevents building AI for AI's sake.
Real-World Application
"Let's add AI to auto-respond to customer support tickets."
1
Generation problem (creating text) + prediction (classifying intent).
2
Wrong answer to a paying customer — high stakes. Need human-in-the-loop or high-confidence thresholds.
3
Non-AI = templates + humans. AI is better for repetitive L1 (password resets, order status), risky for complex complaints.
→Decision: Deploy AI for L1 with high-confidence routing, humans for everything else. This is what Klarna did.

📉
Foundation models are commoditizing
GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, Llama — gaps narrowing. The moat is not the model.
🏗️
Value is moving to the application layer
Best product experience wins. This is where product people matter.
🚀
Enterprise adoption is early but accelerating
Most companies are in pilot phase. Massive opportunity.
🤖

AI that takes actions — books meetings, executes multi-step workflows.
💰

$100 in API calls (2023) → ~$1 now for equivalent quality.

Session 02 · Week 1

The AI Product Landscape

30-40 minutes · Day 2

2.1 Mapping the AI Product Universe

Before you build or market AI products, you need a mental map. The landscape is massive but follows a clear structure. There are four categories — every AI company fits into one, and knowing which shapes your GTM, pricing, and competitive strategy.

2.2 The Four Categories

Category 1
AI-Native Products
Couldn't exist without AI. Remove the AI and nothing's left. ChatGPT, Midjourney, Copilot, Perplexity.
Moat: Data flywheels, user habits, brand trust
Category 2
AI-Enhanced Products
Existing products + AI features. Notion AI, Canva Magic, Salesforce Einstein, Gmail Smart Compose.
Moat: Existing users, distribution, workflow lock-in
Category 3
AI Infrastructure & Tooling
Picks and shovels. Hugging Face, LangChain, Pinecone, Scale AI, AWS Bedrock.
Moat: Developer ecosystem, switching costs
Category 4
Vertical AI Solutions
Built for specific industries. Harvey (law), Abridge (medical), AlphaFold (biotech), Moveworks (IT).
Moat: Domain data, regulatory knowledge, industry trust

2.3 The Three Layers of Players

Layer 1 — Foundation Model Providers

Who: OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, Meta AI, Mistral. Training costs $100M+. Capital-intensive, consolidating. 5-7 major providers globally.

Layer 2 — Cloud & Compute

Who: AWS, Azure, Google Cloud, NVIDIA, CoreWeave. NVIDIA powers ~90% of AI training. Massive business, but not where most product people work.

Layer 3 — Application Layer (Your Target)

Who: Thousands of startups + every SaaS company. Fastest-growing, most accessible. Take models from L1, infrastructure from L2, build products that solve real problems. This is your zone.

2.4 Where the Money Flows

💰
Foundation models get the biggest checks
OpenAI $6.6B at $157B valuation. Anthropic $7.3B+. xAI $6B. Winner-takes-most.
📈
Application layer is where the volume is
Glean ($4.6B), Cursor, Writer — hundreds of well-funded startups. Most new AI product roles live here.
🏢
Enterprise spend is the real story
Every F500 has an AI budget. McKinsey: AI adds $4.4T annually. Biggest spend: integrating AI into workflows.
⚠️
Revenue vs hype gap exists
Many AI startups have high valuations but modest revenue. Winners have clear enterprise ROI, not impressive demos.

2.5 Build vs Buy vs Integrate

🔧The Decision Framework
BUILD — Train or fine-tune in-house
When you have proprietary data as moat, need full control, and have engineering talent. E.g., Netflix recommendations.
BUY — Purchase a vertical AI solution
When a vendor solves your exact problem. Fast deployment, less customization. E.g., law firm buying Harvey.
INTEGRATE — Plug foundation model APIs
Want AI without building models. Most common for startups. E.g., Notion integrating Claude API.

2.6 Five Market Trends

1️⃣
From chatbots to agents
AI that takes actions — Devin (coding), Operator (computer use). Task-completion > Q&A interfaces.
2️⃣
From general to specialized
Vertical AI captures enterprise budgets. A legal AI beats a general chatbot for law firms every time.
3️⃣
From single-model to multi-model
Model routing: cheap model for simple tasks, powerful for complex ones. Cuts costs, maintains quality.
4️⃣
From cloud to edge
On-device AI: Apple Intelligence, Gemini Nano, local LLMs. New categories around privacy and speed.
5️⃣
From "AI feature" to "AI-first redesign"
Cursor didn't add AI to VS Code — they rebuilt the entire editor around AI as the collaborator.

2.7 The AI Product Evaluation Grid

🔍Evaluate Any AI Product
Which of the 4 categories? (Native, Enhanced, Infrastructure, Vertical)
Determines competitive dynamics and GTM.
What's their moat? (Data, distribution, domain, habit, or nothing?)
If the answer is "we use GPT-4" — no moat.
Who pays, and why can't they just use ChatGPT?
The "wrapper" problem. Prettier UI ≠ defensible product.
What happens when models get better and cheaper?
Some become more valuable (platforms). Others go obsolete (thin wrappers).

Session 03 · Week 1

How LLMs Work (No-Code Explanation)

30-40 minutes · Day 3

3.1 What LLMs Actually Do

At its core, a Large Language Model does one thing: predicts the next token. That's it. Every magical-seeming behavior — writing poetry, coding, reasoning — emerges from this one ability applied at massive scale.

When you type "The capital of France is ___", the model doesn't "know" the answer. It calculates probability distributions over its vocabulary and picks the highest-probability next token: "Paris." The magic is that training on trillions of words creates patterns sophisticated enough to seem like understanding.

3.2 The Training Pipeline (Simplified)

1
Pre-training
Feed the model trillions of tokens from the internet — books, code, websites, papers. It learns patterns, grammar, facts, reasoning patterns. Takes months and millions of dollars. This creates a "base model."
2
Fine-tuning (SFT)
Train on curated examples of good conversations. "When asked X, a good response looks like Y." This turns the base model into something that follows instructions instead of just completing text.
3
RLHF / RLAIF
Reinforcement Learning from Human (or AI) Feedback. Humans rank outputs from best to worst. Model learns to prefer responses humans like. This is what makes models helpful, harmless, and honest.

Why This Matters for Product

Each stage has product implications. Pre-training determines the model's knowledge (and biases). Fine-tuning determines behavior and personality. RLHF determines safety and helpfulness. When you evaluate models for your product, you're evaluating all three stages.

3.3 Key Concepts You Must Understand

Context Window
The Model's Working Memory
The total amount of text (in tokens) a model can "see" at once — your prompt + its response. GPT-4 Turbo: 128K tokens (~96K words). Claude: up to 200K tokens. Bigger = handles longer documents but costs more.
Product impact: Determines what your app can process in a single call
Temperature
Creativity vs Precision Dial
Controls randomness. Temperature 0 = always picks the most likely token (deterministic, factual). Temperature 1 = more random picks (creative, varied). For factual apps use low temp, for creative apps use higher.
Product impact: Same prompt, different temperature = different user experience
RAG — Retrieval-Augmented Generation
Giving the Model Your Data
Instead of fine-tuning (expensive, slow), you retrieve relevant documents from your database and paste them into the prompt. The model uses YOUR data to answer, reducing hallucinations. Most enterprise AI products use RAG.
Product impact: How you make AI accurate for your specific domain
Embeddings
How AI Understands Meaning
Text converted into numerical vectors that capture semantic meaning. "Happy" and "joyful" end up close together in vector space. This powers search, recommendations, and RAG retrieval. Stored in vector databases like Pinecone.
Product impact: Enables "find similar" features and smart search

3.4 Why LLMs Hallucinate

This is the single most important thing to understand as a product person building with AI.

LLMs hallucinate because they are pattern completion machines, not knowledge retrieval systems. When the model doesn't have a confident pattern to follow, it generates the most plausible-sounding continuation — which can be completely fabricated. It has no concept of "I don't know."

The Product Implication

You cannot deploy a raw LLM and expect accuracy. Every production AI product needs: guardrails (filter bad outputs), grounding (RAG to provide real data), confidence thresholds (only show output when model is "sure"), and human fallbacks (escalate when uncertain). This is where 80% of AI product engineering effort goes.

3.5 Model Comparison — What to Know

ModelStrengthsBest For
GPT-4oMultimodal, fast, widely integratedGeneral apps, vision tasks
ClaudeLong context (200K), strong reasoning, safetyDocument analysis, enterprise, coding
GeminiGoogle integration, multimodal, large contextGoogle ecosystem, search-heavy apps

Product Person's Take

Don't marry a single model. The smart approach is model-agnostic architecture — design your product so you can swap models easily. Today's best model is tomorrow's commodity. Your value is in the product experience, not the model choice.

Session 04 · Week 1

AI Product vs Traditional Product

30-40 minutes · Day 4

4.1 Why AI Products Break the Rules

Traditional product management assumes deterministic software — the same input always produces the same output. AI products are probabilistic. This single difference cascades into every aspect of how you build, test, launch, and measure.

✕
Traditional: Define specs → Build → Ship → Done
The product works as specified from day one. Bugs are deviations from expected behavior.
✓
AI Product: Define intent → Build → Evaluate → Iterate endlessly
The product's behavior is emergent. "Working correctly" is a spectrum, not binary. You're continuously tuning.

4.2 Seven Ways AI Products Differ

1️⃣
Non-deterministic outputs
Same input → different output each time. Users get inconsistent experiences. You need evaluation frameworks, not just unit tests.
2️⃣
Failure is expected, not exceptional
A 95% accuracy rate means 1 in 20 users gets a wrong answer. Product design must account for graceful failure.
3️⃣
Data is the product (not just code)
Quality of training data, prompt data, and user feedback data directly determines product quality. Data teams = product teams.
4️⃣
Evaluation is hard
How do you measure if a creative writing assistant is "good"? Traditional metrics (latency, uptime) still apply, but quality metrics are subjective and domain-specific.
5️⃣
Trust is the product
Users need to know when to trust AI output and when to verify. Building appropriate trust — not blind trust — is a core UX challenge.
6️⃣
Cost scales with usage differently
Every API call costs money (tokens). A popular feature might 10x your inference costs overnight. Traditional SaaS has near-zero marginal cost per user.
7️⃣
Moats are different
Code moats are weak (anyone can call the same API). Data moats, distribution moats, and workflow moats are strong.

4.3 The AI Product Development Lifecycle

🔄The AI Product Loop
Define the problem + success criteria
What's "good enough"? 90% accuracy? 95%? This is a product decision, not engineering.
Prototype rapidly with existing models
Don't train anything. Use API calls + good prompts to prove the concept works.
Build evaluation frameworks
Automated evals + human evals. This is your testing infrastructure — more important than the model itself.
Ship with guardrails and feedback loops
Launch to small group, collect user feedback, measure quality metrics, iterate on prompts/data/model choice.
Monitor and improve continuously
AI products are never "done." Model drift, changing data, evolving user expectations require ongoing investment.

4.4 Practical Implications for PMs

Setting Expectations

Never promise 100% accuracy. Frame AI features as "assistants" that help users, not "replacements" that work autonomously. Underpromise and overdeliver. If you tell a user your AI is 99% accurate and they hit the 1%, they'll lose all trust.

Pricing Considerations

Token costs are real. Calculate your unit economics carefully. A feature that costs $0.01 per call seems cheap until you have 1M daily users making 10 calls each. That's $100K/day. Consider: rate limits, tiered access, caching strategies.

The Human-in-the-Loop Decision

For every AI feature, decide: Full autonomy (AI decides, user sees result), AI suggests, human approves (co-pilot model), or AI drafts, human edits (collaborative model). Higher stakes = more human oversight. Start with more oversight and reduce as trust builds.

Session 05 · Week 1

The AI Value Chain

30-40 minutes · Day 5

5.1 Understanding the Stack

The AI industry has a layered value chain, similar to how the internet has infrastructure (servers), platforms (cloud), and applications (websites). Understanding where value gets created — and captured — tells you where to build your career and products.

Layer 4: Applications (User-facing products — Notion AI, Harvey, Cursor)
Layer 3: Orchestration (Frameworks — LangChain, LlamaIndex, Semantic Kernel)
Layer 2: Models (Foundation models — GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, Llama)
Layer 1: Infrastructure (Compute — NVIDIA, AWS, Azure, TPUs)

5.2 Where Value Gets Created vs Captured

Here's the counterintuitive insight: the layer that creates the most value doesn't always capture the most profit.

Infrastructure (L1)
High value capture — NVIDIA captures enormous profits
NVIDIA's GPU monopoly gives ~75% gross margins. But this is capital-intensive and concentrated — only 2-3 real players. Not where product people work.
Models (L2)
High costs, uncertain capture
Training frontier models costs $100M+. Revenue is growing (OpenAI ~$4B ARR) but margins are thin due to compute costs. Models are commoditizing — long-term value capture uncertain.
Orchestration (L3)
Enabling layer — modest direct value capture
LangChain, vector databases, eval tools. Essential but often open-source with thin monetization. Value capture through enterprise support, hosted services, or acquisition.
Applications (L4)
Highest potential value capture for most companies
Closest to the customer. Can charge based on business value delivered, not tokens consumed. This is where product-market fit, UX, and domain expertise create durable moats.

Your Takeaway

Unless you're joining NVIDIA or a foundation model lab, your career in AI products will be at Layers 3 and 4. The good news: this is where product skills matter most, and where the majority of AI jobs are being created.

5.3 The Smiling Curve of AI

In economics, the "smiling curve" shows that the most profit goes to the ends of the value chain (components and brand), not the middle (assembly). AI has the same shape:

High Profit

Left end — Compute/chips (NVIDIA): Scarce, monopolistic, hardware moats. Right end — Applications with distribution: Own the customer relationship, charge on value. Grammarly, Canva, Salesforce.

Squeezed Middle

Model providers and tooling companies face pressure from both sides — compute costs squeeze margins from below, and applications commoditize models from above. OpenAI has huge revenue but also huge costs.

5.4 Strategic Positioning — Where Should You Play?

🎯Positioning Decision Framework
Do you have unique data? → Build a vertical AI application
Your data is your moat. No one else can train on your proprietary data. Build domain-specific products.
Do you have distribution? → Add AI to your existing product
If you already have users, AI features increase retention and ARPU. Fastest path to revenue.
Do you have deep technical talent? → Build infrastructure or tools
Developer tools, model serving, evaluation infrastructure. Harder to monetize but acqui-hire potential.
Do you have domain expertise? → Consult, then productize
Help enterprises implement AI, learn their pain points, then build the product that automates what you were doing manually.

Week 1 Exam

Foundations Examination

30 minutes · Covers Sessions 1-5
📝 Section A — Concepts (10 mins)
Short answers. Test your recall.
Q1
Define the difference between predictive AI, generative AI, and analytical AI. Give one corporate example for each.
Model Answer

Predictive AI takes input data and predicts outcomes — Netflix recommendation engine predicts what you'll watch next. Generative AI creates new content from prompts — GitHub Copilot generates code from natural language descriptions. Analytical AI extracts insights from large datasets — JPMorgan's COiN parses thousands of loan agreements to surface key terms and risks.

Q2
What is hallucination? Why does it happen? What are two product-level mitigations?
Model Answer

Hallucination is when AI generates confident but factually wrong output. It happens because LLMs are pattern-completion machines — they predict statistically likely next tokens, not retrieve verified facts. They have no concept of "I don't know." Mitigations: (1) RAG — ground the model's responses in your verified data so it draws from real sources. (2) Confidence thresholds — only surface AI responses when the model's confidence exceeds a set level; below that, escalate to human review.

Q3
Name the four categories of AI products. For each, give one example and explain what their primary moat is.
Model Answer

AI-Native (ChatGPT) — moat is user habit and data flywheel. AI-Enhanced (Notion AI) — moat is existing distribution and workflow lock-in. AI Infrastructure (Pinecone) — moat is developer ecosystem and switching costs. Vertical AI (Harvey for law) — moat is domain data, regulatory expertise, and industry trust.

Q4
Explain the difference between fine-tuning and RAG. When would you choose each?
Model Answer

Fine-tuning = additional training on custom data to change model behavior/knowledge permanently. Choose when: you need the model to adopt a specific style, terminology, or behavior pattern, and you have sufficient training data. RAG = retrieving relevant documents at query time and injecting them into the prompt. Choose when: your data changes frequently, you need verifiable/citable sources, or you want accuracy over a large knowledge base without retraining. Most enterprise products start with RAG because it's faster, cheaper, and more controllable.

Q5
What are the four layers of the AI value chain? Where does the most profit get captured and why?
Model Answer

L1: Infrastructure (NVIDIA, AWS) → L2: Models (OpenAI, Anthropic) → L3: Orchestration (LangChain, Pinecone) → L4: Applications (Notion AI, Harvey, Cursor). The "smiling curve" shows most profit at the ends: Infrastructure (NVIDIA's GPU monopoly, ~75% margins) and Applications (own the customer relationship, charge on business value). The middle (models, orchestration) gets squeezed by compute costs and commoditization.

📝 Section B — Applied Scenarios (10 mins)
Apply frameworks to real situations.
Q6
A hospital wants to add AI that reads radiology scans and flags potential tumors. Apply the 3-Question Filter.
Model Answer

(1) This is primarily an analytical/predictive problem — analyzing images and predicting whether abnormalities are present. (2) If wrong (false negative), a tumor gets missed → patient harm, potential death. If wrong (false positive), unnecessary follow-up tests → anxiety and cost. This is extremely high stakes — requires heavy human oversight and regulatory approval. (3) Non-AI alternative: radiologists reading scans manually. AI is meaningfully better as a second opinion — studies show AI + radiologist outperforms either alone. Decision: Deploy as an assistant that flags suspicious scans for radiologist review, never as autonomous diagnosis.

Q7
An e-commerce startup tells you: "Our moat is that we use GPT-4 for product recommendations." Why is this problematic? What would a real moat look like?
Model Answer

Anyone can call the GPT-4 API — it's not a moat, it's a commodity input. A competitor could replicate this in a day. Real moats would be: (1) Proprietary purchase/browsing data from millions of users that improves recommendations over time (data flywheel). (2) Deep integration into the shopping workflow that makes switching costly. (3) A recommendation algorithm trained on their unique catalog + user behavior that can't be replicated with generic models.

📝 Section C — Mini Case (10 mins)
Read and analyze.
Q8
Company X is a mid-size law firm (200 lawyers). They spend $2M/year on junior associates reviewing contracts. They're considering: (A) Building their own AI contract review system, (B) Buying Harvey, (C) Integrating Claude API with their document system. Which do you recommend and why? Address cost, speed, risk, and long-term positioning.
Model Answer 1 — Buy Harvey

Recommended for most firms. Harvey is purpose-built for legal, understands case law and legal terminology, and has already solved the hardest problems (hallucination in legal context, data privacy). Deployment in weeks. Cost: likely $200K-500K/year — still massive savings vs $2M. Risk: vendor dependency. Long-term: fine if AI isn't your core differentiator.

Model Answer 2 — Integrate Claude API

Good middle ground. Build a RAG system over your contract database, use Claude for analysis. More customization than Harvey, cheaper if you have some technical talent. Cost: $50K-150K/year in API + engineering. Risk: you own the maintenance burden and need to build legal-specific guardrails yourself. Long-term: more control but more investment.

Model Answer 3 — Build In-House

Only if you're planning to become an AI legal tech company. Requires hiring 5-10 ML engineers ($1.5M+/year), 12-18 months to production quality, and ongoing maintenance. Makes sense only if you intend to sell the tool to other firms. For a 200-person law firm, this is almost certainly overkill.

Session 06 · Week 2

Problem-Solution Fit for AI

30-40 minutes · Day 7

6.1 The Biggest Mistake in AI Product Development

The #1 mistake: starting with the technology instead of the problem. "We should use AI for ___" is backwards thinking. The right framing is "We have this problem → does AI solve it better than alternatives?"

Most failed AI projects fail not because the AI didn't work, but because the problem wasn't worth solving with AI in the first place — or a simpler solution would have worked.

6.2 The AI Problem-Fit Scorecard

Score any potential AI use case 1-5 on each dimension. If total is below 15, AI is probably the wrong approach.

📊Score Each Dimension (1-5)
Volume — Is this task done at scale? (thousands+ times)
AI shines with volume. If it's 10 times a week, a human is cheaper.
Pattern — Is there a learnable pattern in the data?
If experts can't agree on the right answer, AI won't either. Clear patterns = good fit.
Tolerance — Can the user tolerate errors?
High error tolerance (content suggestions) = great fit. Zero tolerance (medical dosing) = dangerous.
Data — Is training/reference data available?
No data = no AI. Abundant, clean, labeled data = strong foundation.
Value — Does automation save significant time/money?
If the task takes 10 seconds manually, automating it saves nothing meaningful.

6.3 Good vs Bad AI Use Cases

Great Fit
Email categorization for enterprise
Volume: millions daily ✓ Pattern: clear categories ✓ Tolerance: mislabeling is annoying not catastrophic ✓ Data: abundant email history ✓ Value: saves hours daily per employee ✓
Great Fit
Code review suggestions
Volume: thousands of PRs daily ✓ Pattern: well-documented best practices ✓ Tolerance: developer reviews suggestions ✓ Data: massive code repositories ✓ Value: reduces review time by 40%+ ✓
Poor Fit
Strategic business decisions
Volume: rarely done ✗ Pattern: highly contextual, no clear pattern ✗ Tolerance: wrong strategy = company failure ✗ Data: limited comparable examples ✗ Value: not a volume problem ✗
Poor Fit
Employee performance reviews
Volume: quarterly, manageable ✗ Pattern: highly subjective ✗ Tolerance: employees would rightly distrust AI ✗ Data: sensitive, biased historical data ✗ Value: marginal time savings, massive trust risk ✗

6.4 The "Should This Be AI?" Decision Tree

1
Can a rule-based system solve it?
If simple if/then rules work → don't use AI. Example: routing tickets by keyword → use rules. Understanding ticket intent → use AI.
2
Is the data available and clean?
If no → stop here. Fix your data infrastructure first. AI garbage in = garbage out.
3
What's the cost of being wrong?
Low cost → proceed aggressively. High cost → add human oversight or reconsider entirely.
4
Can you measure success?
If you can't define what "good" looks like, you can't evaluate AI performance. Define metrics first.

Session 07 · Week 2

Prompt Engineering for Product People

30-40 minutes · Day 8

7.1 Why Product People Need Prompt Skills

You're not writing prompts for fun — you're designing the instruction layer of your product. The prompt IS the product logic for AI features. A bad prompt with a great model produces worse results than a great prompt with a mediocre model.

7.2 The Anatomy of a Production Prompt

1
System prompt (role + rules)
Sets the AI's persona, constraints, and behavior guidelines. "You are a customer support agent for Acme Inc. Never discuss competitors. Always offer to escalate."
2
Context (data + background)
Inject relevant information via RAG. "Here is the customer's order history: [data]. Here is our return policy: [policy]."
3
Task (what to do)
Clear, specific instruction. "Respond to the customer's question. If the answer isn't in the provided context, say you'll escalate to a human."
4
Format (how to respond)
Define output structure. "Respond in JSON with fields: answer, confidence (high/medium/low), sources."
5
Examples (few-shot learning)
Show 2-3 examples of ideal input/output pairs. Models learn patterns from examples incredibly well.

7.3 Prompt Design Patterns for Products

Chain-of-Thought
Make the AI show its reasoning
Add "Think step by step" or "Explain your reasoning before giving the answer." Dramatically improves accuracy for complex tasks. The intermediate steps also help you debug when things go wrong.
Self-Critique
Make the AI check its own work
"After generating your response, review it for accuracy. If you find errors or uncertainties, correct them." Adds latency but significantly reduces hallucinations.
Structured Output
Force predictable format
"Respond ONLY in valid JSON with the schema: {intent, confidence, response, escalate}." Essential for building reliable application logic around AI responses.
Persona + Constraints
Define boundaries explicitly
"You are a financial advisor. NEVER give specific stock picks. ALWAYS include a disclaimer. If asked about topics outside finance, politely redirect." Prevents off-brand or dangerous responses.

7.4 Common Prompt Mistakes

✕
Being vague
"Help the customer" → "Resolve the customer's issue using the provided FAQ. If the FAQ doesn't cover it, collect their email and promise a response within 24 hours."
✕
No examples
Models learn best from examples. 2-3 good few-shot examples can improve quality more than paragraphs of instructions.
✕
Ignoring edge cases
What happens when the model doesn't know? When input is in another language? When the user is abusive? Production prompts must handle all of these.
✕
No version control
Prompts are code. Version them, test them, A/B test them. A prompt change can break your product just like a code change can.

Session 08 · Week 2

Evaluating AI Outputs

30-40 minutes · Day 9

8.1 The Eval Problem

In traditional software, testing is binary: the button either works or it doesn't. In AI products, outputs exist on a quality spectrum. A response can be partly right, right but unhelpful, helpful but risky, or technically correct but tonally wrong. Building good evaluation systems is arguably the most important — and most undervalued — skill in AI product development.

8.2 Three Layers of Evaluation

Layer 1
Automated Evals (Fast, Scalable)
Automated metrics that run on every output. Examples: exact match (for factual questions), BLEU/ROUGE scores (for text similarity), format validation (did it return valid JSON?), safety classifiers (flagging toxic content), latency and token usage tracking.
Use for: Regression testing, cost monitoring, safety gates
Layer 2
Model-as-Judge (Medium Speed, Scalable)
Use a second AI model to evaluate the first model's output. "Rate this response 1-5 for helpfulness, accuracy, and relevance." Surprisingly effective when calibrated against human judgment. Cheaper than human eval, more nuanced than automated metrics.
Use for: Quality monitoring at scale, A/B testing prompts
Layer 3
Human Evaluation (Slow, Expensive, Gold Standard)
Domain experts rating AI outputs. Essential for subjective quality (is this legal advice sound?), catching subtle errors machines miss, and establishing ground truth for automated evals. Use a rubric with clear scoring criteria.
Use for: Setting benchmarks, high-stakes domains, calibrating other evals

8.3 Key Metrics for AI Products

MetricWhat It MeasuresWhen to Use
Accuracy% of correct responsesFactual Q&A, classification
Hallucination rate% of responses with fabricated informationAny factual application
User satisfactionThumbs up/down, NPS on AI featuresAll consumer AI features
Task completionDid the AI help the user finish their task?AI assistants, copilots
Fallback rate% of queries that couldn't be handledCustomer support, chatbots
Cost per queryAverage token cost per interactionUnit economics, pricing
Latency p9595th percentile response timeReal-time features

8.4 Building an Eval Pipeline

The Practical Approach

Start with a "golden dataset" — 100-200 curated examples with human-verified ideal answers. Run every prompt change against this dataset. Track metrics over time. When quality drops below threshold, block the deployment. This is your AI product's quality gate, equivalent to automated tests in traditional software.

Session 09 · Week 2

Data Thinking for Non-Engineers

30-40 minutes · Day 10

9.1 Data Is the Moat — Understanding Why

In AI products, code is commoditized (everyone can call the same APIs) but data is unique. Your data determines: what your model knows, how well it performs on your use case, what biases it carries, and whether your product improves over time. As a product person, you don't need to build data pipelines — but you need to think about data strategically.

9.2 The Data Stack for AI Products

1
Training Data
What the base model learned from (mostly internet text). You inherit its knowledge and biases. Important: you don't control this unless you're training your own model.
2
Fine-Tuning Data
Custom examples you use to specialize the model. Quality > quantity. 1,000 excellent examples beat 100,000 mediocre ones. Format: input/output pairs showing ideal behavior.
3
RAG/Knowledge Data
Documents, FAQs, product catalogs — the data your AI references at query time. Must be current, accurate, well-structured. This is usually what product teams own and manage.
4
User Interaction Data
What users ask, what they click, where they give thumbs up/down. This is your flywheel — it tells you what's working, what's failing, and where to improve. Most valuable long-term asset.

9.3 Data Quality — The Make or Break

✕
Bias in, bias out
If your training data over-represents certain demographics, your AI will too. Amazon's recruiting AI famously penalized women's resumes because it learned from historical (biased) hiring data.
✕
Stale data = wrong answers
A product recommendation AI trained on 2023 data won't know about 2025 products. Plan for data refresh cycles.
✕
Incomplete data = blind spots
If your FAQ database doesn't cover a topic, the AI will hallucinate an answer instead of admitting it doesn't know.
✓
The Data Flywheel
Users interact → you collect feedback → improve the model → better product → more users. This is the most defensible moat in AI. Every interaction makes your product smarter.

9.4 Privacy and Compliance

The Non-Negotiables

GDPR / data protection: Users must consent to their data being used for AI training. They have the right to deletion. PII handling: Never train on or retrieve personally identifiable information unless explicitly required. Data residency: Some industries (healthcare, finance, government) require data to stay in specific regions. This affects which AI APIs you can use. Audit trails: In regulated industries, you must be able to explain what data the AI used to make a decision.

  2. What biases might exist in your data and how would you mitigate them?
  3. How would you build a data flywheel that improves recommendations over time?
Session 10 · Week 2

User Research for AI Products

30-40 minutes · Day 11

10.1 AI UX Is Different

Users interact with AI products differently than traditional software. There are unique psychological dynamics: trust calibration (when do I trust this output?), mental model gaps (users often don't understand what AI can/can't do), and the uncanny valley of helpfulness (AI that's 80% helpful can feel worse than no AI at all because users don't know which 20% is wrong).

10.2 The AI Trust Spectrum

Over-Trust
Users blindly accept AI outputs
Dangerous in high-stakes domains. "The AI said to take this dosage" → medical harm. "The AI approved the loan" → financial risk. Product mitigation: add friction, show confidence levels, require human confirmation for important actions.
Under-Trust
Users ignore or second-guess every output
Kills adoption. If a code suggestion tool's output is always rewritten by the developer, it's adding overhead not removing it. Product mitigation: prove value early, show accuracy stats, build trust incrementally.
Calibrated Trust
Users know when to trust and when to verify
The goal. Users understand AI strengths and limitations. They use AI outputs as starting points, not final answers. Product enables this by showing sources, confidence, and edit capabilities.

10.3 Research Methods for AI Products

🔬
Wizard of Oz testing
Before building the AI, have humans pretend to be the AI behind the scenes. Tests whether the workflow works before investing in the tech. Invaluable for validating demand.
📊
Error response studies
Deliberately show users wrong AI outputs and observe reactions. Do they catch errors? Do they blindly accept? This tells you how much guardrailing you need.
🎯
Trust calibration interviews
Ask users: "How confident are you in this AI output?" Then reveal whether it was right or wrong. This measures trust calibration — do users know when to trust?
📈
A/B testing with and without AI
Compare task completion time, quality, and satisfaction between AI-assisted and non-AI workflows. The only way to prove AI actually helps.

10.4 UX Patterns That Build Trust

✓
Show your sources
"Based on your FAQ document, page 12..." — citing sources lets users verify and builds confidence.
✓
Display confidence levels
"High confidence" vs "I'm not sure about this" — transparency about uncertainty is more trustworthy than false certainty.
✓
Make editing easy
AI drafts, human edits. The "suggest → review → accept/modify" loop is the most successful AI UX pattern.
✓
Progressive disclosure
Start with simple AI assistance, gradually introduce more autonomous features as trust builds. Don't go from 0 to full autopilot.
✓
Feedback mechanisms
Thumbs up/down, "was this helpful?", report wrong answers. Users who can give feedback feel more in control and more trusting.
✍️ Session 10 Exercise
You're designing a UX for an AI-powered financial planning tool.
  1. Where on the trust spectrum would your users likely fall? Why?
  2. Design 3 specific UX elements that build calibrated trust.
  3. What research study would you run before launch to validate the AI actually helps users make better financial decisions?
Week 2 Exam

Core Skills Examination

30 minutes · Covers Sessions 6-10
📝 Section A — Concepts (10 mins)
Short answers.
Q1
What are the 5 dimensions of the AI Problem-Fit Scorecard? Give an example of a use case that scores poorly on at least 3 dimensions.
Model Answer

Volume, Pattern, Tolerance, Data, Value. Poor example: AI-generated employee termination letters. Volume: low (infrequent) ✗. Pattern: highly contextual to each situation ✗. Tolerance: enormous legal and emotional risk from errors ✗. Data: sensitive and limited ✗. Value: marginal time savings vs massive risk ✗. Score: ~7/25 — clearly not an AI use case.

Q2
Name the 5 components of a production prompt and explain why each matters.
Model Answer

System prompt (defines role + rules — prevents off-brand behavior), Context (injects relevant data via RAG — grounds responses in facts), Task (clear instruction — focuses the model on the right output), Format (defines structure — makes output parseable by code), Examples (few-shot learning — shows the model what "good" looks like, often more effective than written instructions).

Q3
Explain the three layers of AI evaluation. When would you use each?
Model Answer

Automated evals (format validation, safety classifiers, metrics) — run on every output, fast and scalable, use for regression testing and safety gates. Model-as-Judge (second AI rates first AI's output) — medium speed, use for quality monitoring at scale and A/B testing prompts. Human evaluation (domain experts with rubrics) — slow and expensive but gold standard, use for calibrating other evals, high-stakes domains, and establishing ground truth.

📝 Section B — Applied Scenarios (10 mins)
Q4
A logistics company wants to use AI to predict delivery delays. Apply the Problem-Fit Scorecard (score each dimension 1-5) and make a recommendation.
Model Answer

Volume: 5/5 (thousands of deliveries daily). Pattern: 4/5 (traffic, weather, distance are learnable; rare events harder). Tolerance: 4/5 (a wrong prediction is annoying but not dangerous — customer just gets a wrong ETA). Data: 4/5 (rich logistics data exists — routes, timestamps, weather APIs). Value: 5/5 (accurate ETAs reduce customer complaints and improve routing efficiency). Total: 22/25 — excellent AI fit. Recommendation: proceed. Start with a simple model using historical delivery data + weather, iterate from there.

Q5
Write a system prompt for an AI that handles returns for a consumer electronics company. Include role, at least 2 constraints, and format specification.
Model Answer

System prompt: "You are the returns assistant for TechStore, a consumer electronics retailer. Your role is to help customers initiate returns and exchanges. CONSTRAINTS: (1) Only approve returns within our 30-day return window — if the purchase date is more than 30 days ago, explain the policy and offer to connect them with a manager. (2) Never process refunds above $500 without flagging for human approval. (3) Always verify the order number before proceeding. FORMAT: Respond with a JSON object containing: {action: 'approve'|'escalate'|'deny', reason: string, next_steps: string, requires_human: boolean}."

Q6
You notice users of your AI writing tool are accepting AI suggestions without reading them carefully. What UX changes would you make?
Model Answer

This is an over-trust problem. Solutions: (1) Add a "Review before accepting" step with a brief diff view highlighting what the AI changed — force a cognitive pause. (2) Show confidence indicators: mark suggestions the AI is uncertain about differently (e.g., yellow highlight vs green). (3) Add periodic "accuracy checks" — occasionally ask users to verify a suggestion and reward careful review. (4) Track and surface a personal "correction rate" — "You modified 40% of AI suggestions this week" — normalizing editing behavior. (5) For important documents, require explicit confirmation: "This will be sent to your client. Confirm all AI suggestions are accurate."

📝 Section C — Mini Case (10 mins)
Q7
A SaaS company (B2B project management tool, 50K users) wants to add 3 AI features: (A) AI-generated project status reports from task data, (B) AI that predicts which projects will miss deadlines, (C) AI that auto-assigns tasks to team members. For each: classify the AI type, score problem-fit (quick 1-5 on each dimension), and recommend a launch order with reasoning.
Model Answer 1

Feature A — Status reports: Generative AI. Volume: 4 (weekly per project). Pattern: 5 (clear structure). Tolerance: 4 (draft, human reviews). Data: 5 (all task data exists). Value: 5 (saves 30min/week per PM). Score: 23. Launch first — low risk, high visible value, builds trust in AI features.

Model Answer 2

Feature B — Deadline prediction: Predictive AI. Volume: 4. Pattern: 4 (historical completion patterns exist). Tolerance: 4 (early warning, not a final decision). Data: 4 (need sufficient history). Value: 5 (proactive risk management). Score: 21. Launch second — slightly higher complexity, needs historical data to train on, but very high PM value.

Model Answer 3

Feature C — Auto-assign: Predictive AI. Volume: 5. Pattern: 3 (workload and skill match is nuanced). Tolerance: 2 (wrong assignment causes frustration, political issues). Data: 3 (need skill profiles, availability, preference data). Value: 3 (moderate time savings). Score: 16. Launch last or reconsider — highest risk of backlash. People care deeply about task assignment and may resist AI making these decisions. If launched, start as "suggestions" not auto-assignments.
